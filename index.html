What is Laytime?

Laytime refers to the period agreed upon in the charter party during which the loading and/or discharging operations must be completed. It is usually a defined time frame.

Laytime can be expressed in hours or days and is divided into "working days" or "running hours," depending on the agreement. There can also be terms in the CP that account for weather or other environmental adversaries that pause the counting of the laytime (for example if it rains heavily, cargo Operations stop therefore, laytime counting stops.)

There are variations on how laytime is counted which will be covered in a separate wiki.

Laycan

Laytime is not to be confused with Laycan. Laycan is a portmanteau of "Laydays" and "Cancelling Date". It specifies the time frame within which a ship must arrive at the loading port and be ready to load e.g. 1st to 5th November

Demurrage

Demurrage is a term used in the shipping industry to refer to a financial penalty imposed on the charterer when the laytime is exceeded. It compensates the shipowner for the delay the ship experiences in port beyond the allocated laytime.

Despatch

Despatch is the term used to refer to a payment made by the shipowner to the charterer when the loading or unloading of cargo is completed in less time than the agreed-upon laytime. It serves as an incentive for the charterer to expedite cargo operations.

What is NOR?

NOR stands for "Notice of Readiness." It is a formal declaration given by the master of the vessel to the charterer, indicating that the ship has arrived at the port and is ready to commence loading or unloading of cargo.

Here's a more detailed explanation of its role and significance:

Key Features of NOR:

Commencement of Laytime:

The issuance of NOR is crucial because it typically marks the starting point for laytime, as laid out in the charterparty. This means that once the NOR is accepted, the countdown for completing loading or discharging operations begins.

Conditions for Tendering NOR:

  • The ship must be at the agreed location (within the port or at anchorage as specified in the charterparty).
  • The vessel must be physically ready to load or discharge. This includes having all equipment functioning (which could be hatches open/clean, cranes ready, crew stadning by) and necessary clearances obtained (paperwork from the port agent).

Formalities:

  • NOR must be issued in writing and received by the charterer or their representative to be valid.
  • The specific requirements for the document's format and content are often stipulated in the charterparty agreement.

Acceptance of NOR:

The charterer or the agent must acknowledge receipt of NOR for laytime to start officially. Sometimes, disputes can arise over the acceptance of NOR if the charterer feels the vessel is not truly ready.

Practical Readiness vs. Legal Readiness:

Sometimes, a vessel might be legally ready (documents and permissions in order) but not practically ready (mechanical or logistical issues). Disputes can occur if these conditions are not clear.

Importance of NOR:

Clarity and Documentation:

NOR provides a clear and documented point from which calculations regarding laytime and potential demurrage or despatch can be made.

Operational Coordination:

It ensures all parties are aligned on when port operations should begin, allowing for better scheduling and resource allocation.

Understanding the process and importance of NOR is essential for both charterers and shipowners to ensure smooth and efficient operations at ports, while also protecting against financial liabilities due to timing issues.

What is the Statement of Facts?

A "Statement of Facts" (SoF) is a detailed record of events and activities that occur during a ship's loading or unloading process at a port. It plays a crucial role in laytime calculations and the determination of any demurrage or despatch.

Key Features of a Statement of Facts:

Detailed Chronological Record:

The SoF includes a comprehensive log of all significant events, such as the ship's arrival, tendering and acceptance of the Notice of Readiness (NOR), commencement of loading or discharging, completion of cargo operations, and any delays.

Content:

Typical entries in the SoF might include:

  • Time of arrival at port anchorage or berth.
  • Time and date when NOR is tendered and accepted.
  • Start and end times for loading/unloading.
  • Duration and reasons for any stoppages (e.g., weather, strikes, port closures).
  • Details of any delays and their causes.

Verification:

The SoF is usually verified and signed by the ship's master and the port agent or charterer's representative. This validation is important to ensure that all parties agree on the facts recorded.

Impact on Laytime Calculations:

Reference Document:

The SoF serves as a crucial reference for calculating the laytime used. By detailing when operations started and stopped, it allows for precise computation of the actual laytime expended.

Demurrage and Despatch:

Based on the SoF, parties can determine if the laytime exceeded the agreed-upon duration or if operations were completed sooner.

Dispute Resolution:

The SoF provides an objective record that can be reviewed to resolve issues fairly and transparently.

Accountability:

By documenting delays and their causes, the SoF helps identify who bears the cost of delays.

Laytime Glossary

Key Terms

Charterparty Agreement
This contract outlines all terms, including laytime specifications. It's crucial for defining what is agreed upon regarding time allowed for loading and discharging.
Demurrage
Charges incurred when laytime is exceeded.
Despatch
Incentives paid when loading or discharge is completed in less than the allowed laytime, typically half the demurrage rate.
Exclusions
Pre-agreed exceptions, such as holidays or specific hours when operations can't or won't be performed.
Laytime
The time allowed for loading and discharging cargo without incurring additional costs.
Loading and Discharge Ports
The locations which often have different stipulations for laytime calculations. Port-specific terms can alter the calculation.
Notice of Readiness (NOR)
A notice submitted by the ship to the charterer indicating readiness to load or discharge cargo. The timing of this notice is critical as it often triggers the start of laytime.
Reversible or Irreversible Laytime
Whether laytime can be combined for loading and discharge (reversible) or must be treated separately (irreversible).
Turntime
A period allowed for any operational handovers or preparations, which does not count against laytime.
Weather Clause
Conditions under which certain types of weather may pause the counting of laytime.

Types of Laytime Agreements

Fixed Laytime
A predetermined amount of time specified for loading or unloading operations. For example, a charterparty might stipulate that a ship has 48 hours to complete loading.
Working Days
Laytime is counted using only the typical operating hours or working days at the ports. This excludes weekends, public holidays, or other non-working periods unless otherwise specified.
Weather Working Days (WWD)
Only those days when weather permits work to be done are counted. If adverse weather halts loading or unloading, those days may not count towards laytime.
Running Days
Also known as calendar days, these include all consecutive days, regardless of weekends or holidays.
Working Days of 24 Consecutive Hours
Laytime is measured in blocks of 24-hour periods during which loading or discharging can take place, constrained by operational hours.
Holiday Excluded Days
Excludes defined holidays from the laytime counting, which could include national or port-specific holidays.
Customary Quick Despatch
An open-ended term usually meaning cargo operations should be completed as quickly as reasonably and customarily possible, reflecting the typical speed of operations at a port.
Weather Working Days of 24 Consecutive Hours (WWD of 24 CH)
A specific combination where only those weather conditions allowing work within a full 24-hour period are counted. This considers adverse weather and its impact over complete days.

Common Abbreviations

SHINC
Saturdays, Sundays, and Holidays Included. This means that laytime is counted even on weekends and public holidays.
SHUT
Saturdays, Sundays, and Holidays Unaccounted. This specifies that laytime does not count on these days, effectively pausing the laytime calculation.
HH
Half-Holidays. This indicates that particular days, which may be considered half-holidays, are partially included in the laytime calculations.
WWD
Weather Working Days. This means that laytime is counted only on days when the weather is permissible for loading or unloading operations.
CWH
Calendar Working Hours. This refers to a situation where laytime is counted based on the standard hours for operations and may include weekends or holidays depending on the specific charter terms.
CWD
Calendar Working Days. This term often encompasses days regardless of whether they are standard working days, weekends, or holidays, similar to running days.
LHD
Laytime Hours/Days. It's used in some instances to indicate the manner in which laytime is computed in hours or days.
LSTA
Laytime Saved by the Time Allowed. This indicates the amount of laytime that has been saved based on actual loading or discharging time versus the agreed amount of time.
UWS
Unforeseen Weather Stoppage. It indicates the days deemed unreasonable for operations due to unforeseen weather conditions that will not count against laytime.
